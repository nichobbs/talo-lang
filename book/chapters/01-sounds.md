# Chapter 1 — The sounds and letters

Talo uses twenty letters of the ordinary Latin alphabet. There are no accents, no
extra symbols, and no capital/lowercase rules to worry about — Talo is written in
plain lowercase (we capitalise names and sentence-starts in this book only to help
English-speaking eyes).

## The five vowels

| letter | sounds like | as in |
|---|---|---|
| `a` | *ah* | f**a**ther |
| `e` | *eh* | b**e**d |
| `i` | *ee* | mach**i**ne |
| `o` | *oh* | f**o**r |
| `u` | *oo* | f**oo**d |

Each vowel has **one** sound, always. `a` is never the *a* of "cat" or "name"; it
is always *ah*.

## The fifteen consonants

| letter | sounds like |
|---|---|
| `p t k` | as in **p**en, **t**op, **k**ite |
| `b d g` | as in **b**ed, **d**og, **g**o (g always hard) |
| `c` | *ch* as in **ch**air |
| `f s h` | as in **f**un, **s**un, **h**at |
| `m n` | as in **m**an, **n**o |
| `l` | as in **l**et |
| `w y` | as in **w**et, **y**es |

Two things to notice:

- `c` is always the *ch* sound (never *k* or *s*). So `cao` "goodbye" sounds like
  English *chow*.
- `y` is always a consonant (the *y* of "yes"), never a vowel.

There is **no** `j`, `q`, `r`, `v`, `x`, or `z` in Talo. Words borrowed from
languages that have those sounds are adapted: an *r* becomes `l`, a *j* becomes
`y`, a *v* becomes `w`, a *z* becomes `s`.

## How syllables work

A Talo syllable is built as **(consonant) – vowel – (n)**: an optional consonant,
then one vowel, then optionally the letter `n`. That is the whole rule.

This means:

- every syllable has exactly one vowel as its centre;
- the only consonant that can *close* a syllable is `n`;
- you never get a pile-up of consonants. The only consonant cluster Talo allows
  at all is `n` followed by one of `p t k b d g c`, as in `pintu` "door"
  (pin-tu).

Because of this, Talo words come out evenly paced and easy to say:
`ka-nu`, `ba-i-ti`, `ma-kan`, `pin-tu`.

## Stress

Stress always falls on the **first** syllable of a word. `BA-i-ti`, `MA-kan`,
`KA-nu`. You never have to wonder which syllable to emphasise, and stress never
changes the meaning of a word.


`kepala`.

`tak`, `blu`, `kanu`, `pri`, `sun`.
