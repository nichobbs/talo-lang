# Chapter 4 — Building sentences: who does what to whom

## Dialogue

```talo
A:  Hitoka donato panika kanuka na.   › The person gives water to the dog.
B:  Kanuka manuto baitika lo.         › The dog eats in the house.
A:  Te katato cakulaka kisuka wa.     › He cuts the food with a knife.
```

## Grammar: the subject comes first

Talo has one firm word-order rule: **the subject comes first.** The subject is the
doer — the one performing the action. After that, Talo is relaxed about order,
because the badges already tell you what each word is.

Both of these are correct and mean the same thing:

```talo
Kanuka kanto hitoka.    › The dog sees the person.  (subject–verb–object)
Kanuka hitoka kanto.    › The dog sees the person.  (subject–object–verb)
```

The verb is found by its `-to` badge wherever it sits; the subject is whatever
comes first. The plain object (the thing acted upon) needs no marker — being
*after* the subject is enough.

What you may **not** do is put the verb before the subject:

```talo
* kanto kanuka     › ★ wrong: nothing comes before the verb to be the subject
Kanuka kanto.      › The dog sees.
```

## Grammar: role markers (little words that come after)

When a noun plays a special role — a destination, a location, a tool — Talo marks
it with a small word placed **after** the noun. These are called *postpositions*
(like English prepositions, but they follow the noun instead of preceding it).

| marker | role | English |
|---|---|---|
| `na` | recipient | to (someone) |
| `lo` | location | at, in |
| `su` | direction | toward |
| `fe` | source | from |
| `wa` | instrument | with, using |
| `we` | possession | of (before the head) |

```talo
Mi donato panika yu na.        › I give water to you.
Kanuka tumito baitika lo.      › The dog runs in the house.
Te ikuto kotaka su.            › He goes toward the town.
Mi datanto baitika fe.         › I come from the house.
Te katato cakulaka kisuka wa.  › He cuts the food with a knife.
```

For "of", the possessor comes first with `we`, then the thing owned:

```talo
Mi we kanuka tumito.     › My dog runs.  (literally: I-of dog runs)
```
