# Chapter 3 — People, and the word "to be"

## Dialogue

```talo
A:  Yu yato sela?          › Who are you?
B:  Mi yato Milaka.        › I am Mila.
A:  Mi yato senanpe.       › I am happy.
B:  Te yato edukika.       › He/she is a teacher.
```

## Grammar: the pronouns

Talo has three basic pronouns, with no gender and no formal/informal split:

| Talo | English |
|---|---|
| `mi` | I, me |
| `yu` | you |
| `te` | he, she, it, they |

That is it. One word, `te`, covers "he", "she", "it" and "they" — Talo does not
force you to choose a gender. To make any of them plural, add the little word `pu`
(Chapter 6): `mi pu` "we", `yu pu` "you all", `te pu` "they".

Pronouns are **function words**: they never take a badge.

## Grammar: "to be" is a real verb

To say "X is Y", Talo uses the verb root `ya` with its verb badge: `yato`. It
links a subject to either a **noun** ("X is a dog") or a **modifier** ("X is big"):

```talo
Kanuka yato dobuka.     › The dog is an animal.
Kanuka yato gandepe.    › The dog is big.
Mi yato edukika.        › I am a teacher.
```

This is different from English, which hides "is" inside the grammar. Talo says it
out loud with `yato`, every time. That keeps things unambiguous: `gandepe kanuka`
(no `yato`) is "a big dog" (a thing), while `kanuka yato gandepe` (with `yato`) is
"the dog is big" (a statement).

## Names

A name is treated like any noun: it takes the `-ka` badge when it is doing a
noun's job in the sentence. The name itself is spelled to fit Talo's sounds.

```talo
Milaka datanto.         › Mila comes.
Mi kanto Satoka.        › I see Sato.
```

## Vocabulary

| Talo | English |
|---|---|
| mi / yu / te | I / you / he-she-it-they |
| ya (→ yato) | be |
| senan | happy |
| dobu | animal |
| edu | teach (→ `edukika` teacher, Ch. 7) |
| datan | come |
| kan | see |
