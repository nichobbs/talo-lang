# Chapter 12 — Comparing, describing, and reporting

## Dialogue

```talo
A:  Yu bekito somato.                    › You should study.
B:  Mi tauto ce soma yato muhimupe.      › I know that study is important.
A:  Somaka yato haope lebi uanka fe.     › Study is better than money.
B:  Hi — somakika tauto lebi.            › Yes — those who study know more.
```

## Grammar: comparing with `lebi … fe` ("more … than")

You already met `lebi` "more" and `sukuna` "less". To name **what you are
comparing against** — the "than" part — mark it with `fe` "from":

```talo
Nekoka yato pikope lebi kanuka fe.   › The cat is smaller than the dog.
Mi gandepe lebi yu fe.               › I am bigger than you.
```

Read it as "smaller, from the dog". For the **most** of all, compare against
"all", `ote`:

```talo
Nekoka pikope lebi ote fe.           › The cat is the smallest of all.
```

## Grammar: degree — very, too, only

Three small words come **after** the modifier they scale: `sana` "very", `tai`
"too (excessively)", `dake` "only".

```talo
Kitonka ini kataipe tai.   › This system is too complex.
```

For "**too X to do Y**", follow it with a `sehinga` "so" clause:

```talo
Te osoipe tai, sehinga mi ne bekito tauto te.   › It is too slow, so I cannot follow it.
```

For multiples, use `kai` "times" — `nu kai` "twice / double", and `lebi nu kai fe`
"more than double".

## Grammar: describing with a verb — "the … that …"

To say "the dog **that runs**" or "people **who study**", put the verb in
**modifier** form (`-pe`) after the noun. It then describes the noun like an
adjective:

```talo
kanuka hasilupe       › the running dog / the dog that runs
hitoka somape         › people who study
negalaka tolonape     › a country that helps
```

When the noun is the **object** of the inner action ("the dog I saw"), say it as
two short clauses, picking the noun up again with `te`:

```talo
Mi kanto kanuka. Te hasiluto li.   › The dog (that) I saw ran.
```

## Grammar: fuller relatives with `seko`, `sela`, `selo` …

When the inner clause has more than one word, the `-pe` shortcut is not enough.
Put the head noun first, then a **correlative** — `seko` (which), `sela` (who),
`selo` (where), `seno` (when) — to introduce the clause. It stands for the head
noun inside it:

```talo
Mi kanto honka seko yu bacato li.    › I see the book which you read.
Mi tauto hitoka sela tolonato yu.    › I know the person who helped you.
```

These are the same question-words from Chapter 5, reused here as relative
connectors — no new word to learn.

## Grammar: must, should, can

Talo has three plain "helping" verbs. Put one **before** the main verb, and the
main verb keeps everything else:

| Talo | meaning |
|---|---|
| `lasima` | must / have to |
| `beki` | should / ought |
| `bisa` | can / be able |

```talo
Mi bekito somato.            › I should study.
Yu bisato datanto.          › You can come.
Te lasimato makanto.        › He must eat.
Totoka bekito bacato honka. › Children should read books.
```

To say *should not / cannot*, put `ne` before the helping verb:

```talo
Te ne bekito tumito.        › He should not run.
```

## Grammar: reporting with `ce` ("that")

To report what someone says, knows or thinks — to put a whole clause **inside**
another — join them with `ce` "that":

```talo
Mi tauto ce yu datanto li.        › I know that you came.
Te semato ce panika kunato.       › He says that there is water.
Mi omouto ce yu yato benape.      › I think that you are right.
```

For a direct quotation you can still just place the two clauses side by side
(Chapter 10); `ce` is for tucking one clause inside another as its object.

## Grammar: "as" and "like"

English "as/like" splits three ways in Talo, each with its own word:

- **manner** ("*as* you said", "the way you do it") — use `sewa`, the "how" word:
  ```talo
  Te tendato sewa mi semato.   › He did as I said.
  ```
- **similar to / like** — use `sadisape` "similar" or `samape` "same" as an ordinary
  describing word:
  ```talo
  Nekoka yato sadisape kanuka wa.   › The cat is like (similar to) the dog.
  ```

## Grammar: "whether" — a yes/no question inside a clause

To tuck a **yes/no question** inside another clause ("I don't know **whether** it is
true"), wrap it in `ce` … `ke` — the reporting `ce` plus the question word `ke`:

```talo
Mi ne tauto ce te yato benape ke.   › I don't know whether it is true.
```

## Vocabulary

| Talo | English |
|---|---|
| lebi … fe | more … than |
| ote | all (… than all = the most) |
| -pe (on a verb) | -ing / that … (describing word) |
| lasima | must |
| beki | should |
| bisa | can |
| ce | that (reporting) |
| ce … ke | whether (a yes/no question inside a clause) |
| seko / sela | which / who (relative connector) |
| sewa | as / the way (manner) |
| sadisa (→ sadisape) | similar / alike |
| sama (→ samape) | same |
| muhimu | important |
| bena | right, true |

**12.2** Say "people who help" and "the eating cat." (help `tolona`, eat `makan`)

**12.3** Translate, using a helping verb: "You should come" and "I cannot eat."
