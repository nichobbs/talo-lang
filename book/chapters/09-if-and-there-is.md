# Chapter 9 — "If", and "there is"

## Dialogue

```talo
A:  Fi panika kunato, mi pinato.       › If there is water, I drink.
B:  Panika kunato baitika lo.          › There is water in the house.
A:  Mi kunato inilo.                 › I am here.
```

## Grammar: conditions with fi

To make an "if" sentence, begin the condition with `fi`, then state the result as
an ordinary clause:

```talo
Fi yu datanto, mi yato senanpe.   › If you come, I am happy.
```

The result clause needs nothing extra. If you want to underline it, you may begin
it with `toki` "then":

```talo
Fi yu belayato, toki yu tauto lebi.   › If you learn, then you know more.
```

Because Talo has no tenses, the same `fi` covers "if you come" and "if you came" —
context (or a time-word) tells which.

## Grammar: unreal conditions with `andai`

For an **imaginary or contrary-to-fact** condition — "supposing…", "if only…", "if
it *had* happened…" — begin with `andai` instead of `fi`. The "unreal" sense lives
in `andai` itself; you do not need any other marker:

```talo
Andai mi motuto uanka, mi belito baitika.   › If I had money, I would buy a house.
```

Add `li` or a time-word if you need to place the supposed event firmly in the past.

## Grammar: ranges ("from X to Y")

For a range of numbers, put the two bounds around `su` "to", **after** the noun:

```talo
Mi subilito dinka mo su le.   › I waited three to five days.
```

The noun comes first: `dinka mo su le` = "days, 3 to 5". (Putting the numbers before
the noun is not allowed — determiners always follow.)

## Grammar: "there is" / "to be located" with kuna

The verb `yato` (Chapter 3) links a subject to a description. It does **not** do
"there is" or "X is in a place". For those, Talo has a separate verb, `kuna`
"exist / be located":

```talo
Panika kunato.              › There is water.
Bala kunato baitika lo.     › There is someone in the house.
Kanuka kunato baitika lo.   › The dog is in the house.
Mi kunato inilo.            › I am here.
```

It behaves like any verb — you can negate it or question it:

```talo
Panika ne kunato.             › There is no water.
Bala kunato baitika lo ke?  › Is anyone in the house?
```

> **Two "be" verbs, two jobs.** Use `yato` for "X is (a) Y" and "X is
> (quality)". Use `kunato` for "there is X" and "X is at/in a place". Keeping them
> separate is what makes Talo clear about which kind of statement you mean.

## Vocabulary

| Talo | English |
|---|---|
| fi | if |
| toki | then |
| kuna (→ kunato) | exist, be located |
| pina | drink |
| lebi | more |
| mungi | maybe |

## Exercises

**9.1** Translate: "If you eat, I am happy."

**9.2** Say "There is a dog in the house."

**9.3** Say "I am here" and ask "Is there water?"

**9.4** Which verb — `yato` or `kunato` — for each: "The cat is small." /
"The cat is in the house."
