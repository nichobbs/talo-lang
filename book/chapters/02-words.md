# Chapter 2 — Words and the three badges

## Dialogue

```talo
# Two people look at an animal.
A:  Inila yato seko?     › What is this (thing)?
B:  Itu yato kanuka.     › That is a dog.
A:  Kanuka tumito!       › The dog is running!
```

(`kanu` dog, `tumi` run, `ya` be, `ini`/`itu` this/that.)

## Grammar: roots wear badges

In Talo, a bare word-root has a meaning but **no part of speech**. The root `kanu`
just means "dog-ness"; `tumi` means "running-ness". To use a root in a sentence,
you add a one-syllable ending — a **badge** — that says what *kind* of word it is
right now:

| badge | makes it a… | example |
|---|---|---|
| `-ka` | noun (a thing) | `kanuka` "a dog" |
| `-to` | verb (an action) | `tumito` "runs / to run" |
| `-pe` | modifier (describes) | `tumipe` "running, fast" |

The badge is **always the last syllable** of the word. This one fact is the engine
of the whole language: you can always tell what job a word is doing by looking at
its ending.

So from a single root you get three words for free:

```talo
# manu (eat):
# manuka  — a meal / eating (noun)
# manuto  — eats / to eat   (verb)
# manupe  — eating / edible (modifier)
```

Every content word **must** wear a badge. A bare root is not a usable word:

```talo
* kanu tumito     › ★ wrong: 'kanu' has no badge
Kanuka tumito     › The dog runs.
```

(The starred line is wrong because `kanu` is missing its `-ka`.)

## Naming things: the noun badge -ka

Add `-ka` to any root to name it:

```talo
# pani (water) → panika  "water"
# baiti (house) → baitika "house"  ... wait — see the note below
```

> **A note on built-in endings.** Some roots already end in something that *looks*
> like a badge but isn't — `baiti` "house" just happens to end in *-ti*. You still
> add the real badge: `baitika` "a house". The badge is always *added*, never
> assumed.

## Doing things: the verb badge -to

Add `-to` to make an action:

```talo
Mi manuto.        › I eat.
Yu tumito.        › You run.
Kanuka manuto.    › The dog eats.
```

`mi` is "I", `yu` is "you". These little words are special (Chapter 3) and never
take a badge.

## Describing things: the modifier badge -pe

A modifier is a describing word — what English splits into adjectives ("big dog")
and adverbs ("runs fast"). Talo uses one badge, `-pe`, for both. A modifier comes
**before** the word it describes:

```talo
Gandepe kanuka tumito.   › The big dog runs.
```

(`gande` big.) Notice `gandepe` comes before `kanuka`.
