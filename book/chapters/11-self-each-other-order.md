# Chapter 11 — Self, each other, and putting things in order

## Dialogue

```talo
A:  Mukuka sendi datanto.        › The chief himself came.
B:  Te i mi tolonato salin.      › He and I help each other.
A:  Mi kanto te mo kai.          › I have seen him three times.
B:  Hitoka tape datanto li.      › The first person has arrived.
```

## Grammar: `sendi` — "-self"

`sendi` points an action back at the doer, or simply emphasises a noun. As an
**object**, it means "-self":

```talo
Te kanto sendi.        › He sees himself.
Mi tolonato sendi.     › I help myself.
```

Placed right after a noun or pronoun, it **emphasises** it ("…-self / the very…"):

```talo
Mukuka sendi datanto.  › The chief himself came.
Yu sendi tauto.        › You yourself know.
```

`sendi` never changes shape — the same word covers myself, yourself, himself,
herself, itself, ourselves and themselves. Who it refers to is simply the subject
of the clause.

## Grammar: `salin` — "each other"

`salin` is the **reciprocal**: the people in the subject do the action *to one
another*.

```talo
Te tolonato salin.     › They help each other.
Mi i yu kanto salin.   › You and I see each other.
```

Note the difference: `sendi` turns the action back on the *same* doer; `salin`
sends it *between* the members of a plural subject.

## Grammar: counting in order — `NUM + -pe`

A plain number *counts* ("two books" = `honka nu`). To say **which one in order**
— first, second, third — add the modifier badge `-pe` to the number:

```talo
honka tape    › the first book     (ta "one"  + -pe)
honka nupe    › the second book    (nu "two"  + -pe)
honka mope    › the third book     (mo "three"+ -pe)
```

So a bare number answers "how many?"; a number with `-pe` answers "which?".

## Grammar: "times" with `kai`, and fractions with `bagi`

Two small markers extend the numbers further:

- **`kai`** after a number means "times": `nu kai` "twice", `mo kai` "three times".

  ```talo
  Mi datanto nu kai.     › I came twice.
  Te makanto mo kai.     › He eats three times.
  ```

- **`bagi`** between two numbers makes a **fraction** ("a divided by b"):

  ```talo
  ta bagi mo             › one third   (1 ÷ 3)
  nu bagi le             › two fifths
  ```

  The everyday word `setenga` "half" is kept for its own sake:

  ```talo
  Mi pinato panika setenga.   › I drank half the water.
  ```

## Vocabulary

| Talo | English |
|---|---|
| sendi | -self (reflexive / emphatic) |
| salin | each other (reciprocal) |
| -pe (on a number) | -th (ordinal: first, second, …) |
| kai | times (twice, three times, …) |
| bagi | divided by (fractions) |
| setenga | half |
