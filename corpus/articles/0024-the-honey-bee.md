# The honey bee (a reference entry)

- **Register:** expository / reference — an encyclopedia-style "what is X" entry.
- **Source:** original, written as a normal reference entry, then translated.
- **What it stress-tests (new to the corpus):** the **generic / gnomic present**
  (plain verbs, no aspect markers, for general truths — "bees make honey");
  **definitions** (`X yato Y` "a bee is a small insect"); **classification** and
  quantification (`kila` each, `ingi`/`badi` many/some, `ote`); and the **canonical
  postposed caritive** `Nukika tanpa` "without bees" (per the updated book).
- **Gaps found & handled:** the natural-science lexicon was deep — `nuki` bee,
  `musi` insect, `latu` queen, `mania` worker, `hana` flower, `tubu` plant, `tane`
  seed, `asali` honey, `kiota` nest, `beba` carry, `noen` farm, `taio` sun existed.
  Two genuine gaps filled: **`alami` "nature"** (PHY-094) and **`poleni` "pollen"**
  (AGR-067); "nectar" is paraphrased as `panika manipe hanaka fe` (a flower's sweet
  water); "hive" as `kiotaka` (nest); "winter" as `kisesuka holope` (cold season).

## English

The honey bee is a small insect. Bees live together in large groups; each group has
one queen, many workers, and some males.

Bees get their food from flowers. They drink the flower's sweet water — the nectar —
and they carry its pollen. From the nectar they make honey, and they store the honey
in their nest as food for the cold season.

Bees are very important to nature. When a bee moves from flower to flower, it carries
pollen, and so it helps the plants make seeds and fruit. Without bees, many plants
could not grow.

In recent years, the number of bees has fallen. Scientists say this is dangerous for
nature and for farming.
