# The lion and the mouse (a fable)

- **Register:** children's fable — simple narrative + dialogue + a moral.
- **Source:** an Aesop fable, retold in plain original wording, then translated.
- **What it stress-tests:** **simple past narrative** at an easy reading level;
  **dialogue** inside narration; **repetition** (`pikope`/`gandepe` small/great as a
  refrain); a closing **gnomic moral**; everything kept short — ideal for the
  first-second-language learner.
- **Gaps found & filled:** `winda` "hunt" (ACT-155 → hunter `windakika`). The rest
  existed (`singa` lion, `cuca` mouse, `lema` weak, `kao` face, `yadi` hand/paw,
  `yalae` net, `talia` rope, `umai` bite, `telia` shout → "roar", `coda` let-go).
  "Escape" = `kelua` (leave), "even" = `ti`, "moral" = `belayaka` (the lesson).

## English

One day, a big lion was sleeping under a tree. A small mouse ran across his face and
woke him. The lion caught the mouse in his paw.

"Please let me go," said the mouse. "One day I will help you."

The lion laughed. "How can a little mouse help a great lion?" But he let the mouse go.

Some days later, hunters caught the lion in a strong net. The lion roared, but he
could not escape.

The little mouse heard him. She came, and bit the ropes one by one, until the lion
was free.

"You see?" said the mouse. "A small friend can be a great friend."

The lesson: even the weak can help the strong.
