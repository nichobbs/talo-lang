## English

Scientists have identified a critical early stage of Alzheimer's disease where
intervention may slow or halt cognitive decline, offering what researchers describe
as a narrow but significant window for treatment before irreversible brain damage
occurs.

The discovery, published this week in a major neurology journal, centres on a
buildup of abnormal proteins in the brain that can now be detected years before
patients show any symptoms of memory loss or confusion.

"We are moving away from treating the disease once it has already damaged the brain,"
said Professor James Chen, a neurologist at a Cambridge research institute. "If we
can catch it at this stage, we may be able to prevent it from progressing at all."

### A new diagnostic tool

The breakthrough came through development of a new blood test that measures levels of
phosphorylated tau, a protein long suspected of playing a central role in the
disease's progression. Unlike current methods, which require expensive brain imaging
or invasive procedures, the test costs around £200 and produces results within days.

Trials involving over 3,000 participants showed the test could identify people at
risk of developing Alzheimer's ten to fifteen years before any cognitive symptoms
appeared. Researchers then treated some of these patients with experimental antibody
drugs designed to clear the harmful proteins from the brain.

"In the early-stage group, we saw a 40 per cent slowing of cognitive decline
compared to those who received a placebo," said Dr Maya Patel, who led the clinical
trial. "That is a significant result for a disease we have struggled to treat."

### Cautious optimism

However, researchers emphasise that the findings, while promising, do not yet
constitute a cure. The treated patients still showed cognitive decline over time,
merely at a slower rate. Longer-term studies will be needed to establish whether the
benefits persist or whether resistance to the drugs develops.

There are also practical questions about how to implement mass screening programmes.
If the blood test were offered to all adults over a certain age, millions would be
identified as at-risk, yet only a fraction would ever develop the disease.

"We need to be careful not to medicalize ageing," cautioned Professor Chen. "Not
everyone with these protein markers will develop dementia. The psychological impact
on patients of being told they are at risk could itself cause harm."

The National Health Service said it was monitoring the research closely but had not
yet made any decisions about introducing the test into routine clinical practice.
Private healthcare providers have already begun offering it to paying patients.

Pharmaceutical companies have announced they are accelerating development of drugs
targeting tau protein, with at least five compounds now in advanced clinical trials.
Analysts expect several new treatments could reach the market within two to three
years.

For patients and families affected by Alzheimer's disease, the research offers a rare
note of optimism in a field that has seen repeated failures and setbacks. Whether
that optimism can be sustained depends on results from the next phase of trials,
expected within eighteen months.
