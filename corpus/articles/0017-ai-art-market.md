## English

The art world is facing an unprecedented crisis as artificial intelligence tools
capable of creating original paintings, sculptures and installations in seconds
flood the market with cheap alternatives, museum curators and artists'
representatives warn.

Major auction houses have begun refusing to handle works created entirely by
artificial intelligence, whilst galleries struggle to distinguish between human-made
and machine-generated pieces. The uncertainty is already affecting prices for
emerging artists and threatening long-established career paths.

"We are witnessing a fundamental shift in how art is valued," said Eleanor
Blackwell, director of a prominent London contemporary art gallery. "If a competent
painting can be generated for £50, why would a collector pay £5,000 for work by an
unknown human artist?"

### The technology accelerates

The issue has intensified rapidly over the past eighteen months as AI image
generation tools have become both more sophisticated and more accessible. Artists
can now produce portfolio-quality work without formal training. Commercial operators
are generating thousands of images daily for social media, marketing materials and
book illustrations.

The impact has been particularly acute in illustration and commercial art, where
demand has dropped sharply. Several prominent illustrators' representatives reported
a 60 per cent fall in commission inquiries since the technology became widely
available.

"Young illustrators starting out today face a very different landscape from five
years ago," said David Okonkwo, head of a London-based artists' agency. "Many are
struggling to find work at any price."

### Questions of authenticity

Art institutions have begun grappling with fundamental questions about what
constitutes art. A painting created by algorithm raises uncomfortable questions about
authorship, intentionality and the nature of human creativity itself.

The Royal Academy of Arts convened a panel last month to discuss whether
AI-generated works should be eligible for exhibition or prizes. No consensus emerged.
Some argued that the tools are merely a new medium, comparable to photography or
digital art. Others contended that art requires human agency and decision-making in
a way that algorithmic generation does not.

"A photograph required a photographer, even if a machine operated the shutter," said
Professor Marcus Webb, an art historian at Oxford University. "But what is the
creative agent in an AI image? The person who wrote the prompt? The person who
trained the model? The engineer who built the system? The answer is genuinely
unclear."

### The legal vacuum

Lawyers are equally uncertain. Copyright authorities in Britain and Europe are still
determining whether AI-generated works can be copyrighted at all. In the United
States, the Copyright Office has ruled that such works lack the human authorship
necessary for protection, though the decision is being challenged.

Meanwhile, artists are pursuing legal action against the companies that trained their
models on millions of copyrighted paintings without permission or compensation.
Several cases are now before the courts.

"The technology was developed by feeding AI systems vast numbers of existing
artworks," said Rebecca Torres, a lawyer specialising in artistic copyright. "The
question of whether that constitutes fair use, or theft, has not yet been settled."

### Resistance and adaptation

Some artists are fighting back. Several prominent painters have begun prominently
labelling their work as "human-made" or "created without AI assistance," treating it
almost as a quality guarantee. A few galleries are experimenting with higher prices
for authenticated human work, banking on collector preference for genuine human
creativity.

Others are exploring how to incorporate AI as a tool within their practice, much as
artists adopted photography or computer graphics decades ago. The results are mixed,
and many traditionalists view the approach with scepticism.

For now, major museums remain committed to human artists, with curators insisting
that authentic creativity remains central to their mission. But as the technology
advances and the market evolves, that position may become harder to maintain.

"We are in uncharted territory," said Ms Blackwell. "How the art world responds over
the next few years will shape what art means for generations to come."
