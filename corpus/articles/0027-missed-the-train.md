# The day I missed the train (a personal anecdote)

- **Register:** first-person narrative / anecdote вЂ” a remembered story with a moral.
- **Source:** original, written as a normal anecdote and then translated.
- **What it stress-tests (discourse-level, not single-clause):** **anteriority /
  aspect** вЂ” `li` for "had stopped / had forgotten", `wi` for "was leaving";
  **temporal subordination** with `sa` "when / while"; **sequence** (`toki`/`i`,
  `keso` "later"); **reported speech**; a closing **gnomic moral** (general present).
- **Gaps found & filled:** two high-frequency everyday verbs вЂ” `bangu` "wake"
  (BOD-098) and `lupa` "forget" (COG-093). The rest existed (`mutano` meeting,
  `tokei` clock, `gauni` dress, `pakala` catch в†’ "miss the train" = `ne pakalato`,
  `dudu` sit, `isu` chair, `pelan` war, `kadan` sometimes, `inga`/`lupa` remember/
  forget).

## English

Last year, I had to travel to the city for an important meeting. That morning I woke
up late, because my clock had stopped in the night. I dressed quickly and ran to the
station, but I arrived just as the train was leaving. I had missed it by one minute.

I sat on a chair and waited for the next train. While I waited, an old man sat down
beside me and began to talk. He told me his life, his children, and a war he had seen
when he was young. When the next train came, an hour later, I had forgotten all about
my meeting.

That day I learned something: sometimes very good things happen when our plans go
wrong.
