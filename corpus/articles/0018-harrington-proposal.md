## English

It is a truth universally acknowledged that a gentleman in possession of a
considerable fortune must be in want of a wife, though the lady in question may
harbour rather different sentiments on the subject.

Miss Catherine Ashford had reached the age of seven-and-twenty without experiencing
any particular inclination towards matrimony, a fact which caused her mother
considerable distress and her father quiet amusement. The latter had made his
fortune in trade—a circumstance which some of the county's more elevated families
found difficult to overlook—whilst the former remained eternally convinced that her
daughter's unmarried state represented a personal failure of management.

"You cannot mean to refuse him," Mrs Ashford declared, her face flushed with
indignation. "Mr Harrington has called three times this week. The whole
neighbourhood must suppose an understanding already exists between you."

Catherine set down her needlework with deliberate care. "Then the whole
neighbourhood will be disappointed, Mama. Mr Harrington is an amiable man. He is not
without sense or humour. But I have no wish to marry him."

"No wish!" her mother exclaimed. "What has wish to do with anything? Mr Harrington
has an estate in Derbyshire, a townhouse in London, and an income of eight thousand
a year. These are the considerations that ought to occupy a rational mind."

"My mind, it seems, is determined to be irrational," Catherine replied. "For I find
that none of these things incline me towards a life spent in his company."

Her father, observing from behind his newspaper, permitted himself a smile. He had
married for affection, and harboured a private conviction that his daughter had
inherited his peculiar outlook.

Mr Harrington was not an unprepossessing figure. He was tall, well-formed, and rich
enough to render any minor defects entirely immaterial. He had spent three weeks in
a state of agitation, convinced that Miss Ashford's refusals to walk with him were
merely the product of feminine modesty.

"She is playing at reluctance," he confided to his friend over brandy. "She wishes
to secure the best terms before accepting the inevitable. What woman would refuse
such an establishment?"

"And if she is not playing?" his friend ventured quietly. But Mr Harrington found
the suggestion so improbable that he rejected it entirely.

He was therefore startled when Catherine's father called upon him the following
morning to inform him, with grave courtesy, that his daughter could not be induced
to accept his proposal. "Her mind is quite made up," Mr Ashford said, "and it is not
a mind easily altered."

Mrs Ashford wept for a fortnight. She wrote to her cousins and her sister and an
ancient aunt, each letter couched as a personal tragedy: Catherine had thrown away
an advantageous marriage through nothing more than stubborn fancy.

Catherine herself remained unmoved. She had discovered, through careful observation,
that marriage seemed to render women either insipid or bitter, and the risk appeared
altogether too considerable. Instead she occupied herself with reading, with her
correspondence, and with long walks across the countryside.

It was on one such walk, three months later, that she met Mr Edward Fairfax upon the
bridge that crossed the stream between their properties. He was observing the water
with genuine interest. "You are studying the flow," Catherine observed, "as though
it held some particular significance."

"The water is higher than last week," he said simply. "I am trying to determine
whether we shall have flooding in the lower meadows." They spoke of estate
management, the local birds, and the peculiar autumn light. When they parted, Mr
Fairfax expressed the hope of meeting her again, and Catherine, to her slight
surprise, found that she shared it.

Six months later, when Mr Fairfax proposed—in a manner so straightforward as to
contain no artifice—Catherine accepted him with perfect equanimity.

Mrs Ashford experienced a crisis of considerable proportions. Mr Fairfax was
genteel, certainly, but his fortune was modest and he intended to remain upon his
estate rather than maintaining an establishment in town. "How can you marry a man of
such limited prospects?" she demanded.

"Very easily, Mama," Catherine replied. "I have accepted him because I find him
agreeable, because he does not trouble himself with the opinions of society, and
because he expects me to possess a mind of my own and to use it. These qualities
weighed more heavily with me than eight thousand a year."

Her father, upon hearing the engagement, smiled quietly and settled upon his
daughter a sum considerably larger than was necessary. He had always suspected
Catherine would discover what she wanted, and he approved her choice.

Mr Harrington, when he heard, expressed considerable bewilderment. "Perhaps," his
friend suggested, "she simply did not wish to marry you." But Mr Harrington found
this so improbable that he contented himself with the belief that Miss Ashford had
made a grave mistake she would come to regret. He was, as it happened, quite wrong.
But he never knew it.
