# At the market, and asking the way (a conversation)

- **Register:** everyday spoken dialogue — the first conversational piece in the
  corpus. Buying fruit, haggling, paying, then asking directions.
- **Source:** original, written as natural speech first and then translated (not
  reverse-engineered from known-translatable vocabulary).
- **What it stress-tests (grammar the news register never touches):**
  - **imperatives / directions** as 2nd-person `yu` + verb (`Yu calato sugupe…`);
  - **content questions in place** — `semu` "how much", `selo` "where";
  - **requests** — `Yu bekito … ke` "could you…?";
  - **prices** (numeral + `ponka`), **degree** (`kidogo` a bit, `sana` very),
    **interjections** (`pole`, `oke`, `tanku`, `kalibu`, `no`).
- **Gaps found & handled:** the everyday lexicon turned out nearly complete
  (`kiha` train, `eki` station, `kili`/`kanan` left/right, `mawalu` turn,
  `cikai`/`toi` near/far, `muko` bag, `moneda` coin, `meni` minute, `yohali` noon,
  `tabi` journey). The one genuine content gap — **`pear`** — is filled this batch
  as `pila`. "Pence" is avoided by pricing in whole pounds; "excuse me" uses `pole`.

## English

**A:** Excuse me — how much are the oranges?
**B:** One pound each. Or, if you buy six, five pounds.
**A:** That's a bit expensive. Could you make them cheaper?
**B:** For you, six oranges for four pounds. They are very fresh — I picked them this morning.
**A:** Alright. I'll take six oranges. Do you have apples too?
**B:** Sorry, the apples are finished. But the pears are very good today.
**A:** No, thank you. I just want the oranges. Here are four pounds.
**B:** Thank you. Here is your change, and a bag.
**A:** Thank you. One more question — where is the station?
**B:** Walk straight along this road, then turn left at the bridge.
**A:** Is it far?
**B:** Not very far. On foot it is about ten minutes. If you walk quickly, you will arrive before noon.
**A:** Thank you very much for your help.
**B:** You're welcome. Have a good journey!
